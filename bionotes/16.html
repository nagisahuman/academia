Phenotype Distribution

The phenotypes in a population are impacted by natural selection. This can happene in three ways.

  • Directional selection causes the phenotype distribution to shift in a particular direction on a bell curve.
  • Stabilizing selection causes one phenotype in the middle of a bell curve to increase.
  • Disruptive selection causes to unique phenotypes in a population.

Genetic Equilibrium

The Hardy‐Weinberg principle states that allele frequencies are consistent in a population unless one particular factor changes. Genetic equilibrium is when allele frequencies remain constant. These factors impacting genetic equilibrium are:

  • Random mating
  • No movement into or out of a population
  • Large population
  • No mutations
  • No natural selection

Speciation

Speciation is the creation of a new species. This begins with a portion of a population becoming isolated. This isolation can be behavioral, geographic, or temporal.

  • Behavioral isolation occurs when mating behaviors change.
  • Geographic isolation occurs when part of the population is physically separated from the rest of the population.
  • Temporal isolation occurs when parts of a population mate at different times.

Once a species is separated by one of these factors, the gene pool will change. The changes in a gene pool eventually lead to the members of the original population not being able to breed with each other. A species is only made up of members that can interbreed. As these two populations cannot breed with each other, a new species has been created.
