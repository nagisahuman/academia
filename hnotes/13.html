Conflict

As more Americans began moving west and taking control of more land, they began to anger the Native Americans. They repeatedly broke promises, and killed a number of people. Wounded Knee and Little Bighorn are a few examples of the many conflicts between the Native American people and the United States.

Little Bighorn

Several settlers were violating a treaty by using the Black Hills in the Lakota Sioux reservation. The Lakota opposed this by leaving the reservation. In response to this, Colonel George Custer attacked them. However, the Native Americans won against this attack.

Wounded Knee

The Lakota continued to perform the Ghost Dance, which celebrated a hopeful day in which the buffalo would return and the settlers would disappear. The government agents did not approve of this, and sent police to arrest Sitting Bull, the chief, who was killed. Those who partook in the Ghost Dance fled, but many of them were killed by U. S. tropps.
This event took place at Wounded Knee Creek.

Government Plans

Indian Peace Commission

The Indian Peace Commission was formed in 1867 to create reservations for the Native Americans. However, Native Americans were forced into signing these treaties. The reservations did not accomplish anything beyond increasing tensions.

Assimilation

To assimilate the Native Americans would mean to absorb them into the standard American culture. They were given small amounts of land which they were allowed to use for farming and to live off of. However, the land they were given was not profitable, and this idea ultimately failed.

Indian Boarding schools were also created to "e;Kill the Indian, Save the Man."e; These schools forced children to choose new names ‐ names that they could not read and did not know the meaning of. Their hair was cut. They were not allowed to speak their native languages. Their clothing changed entirely, and they could not dress how they used to. Everything related to their culture was removed from them.
